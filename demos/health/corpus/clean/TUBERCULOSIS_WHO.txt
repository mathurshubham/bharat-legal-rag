TUBERCULOSIS — WHO FACT SHEET

Key Facts

A total of 1.23 million people died from tuberculosis in 2024, including 150,000 among people with HIV. TB ranks as the world's leading cause of death from a single infectious agent and is among the top 10 causes of death globally.

In 2024, an estimated 10.7 million people fell ill with TB worldwide, comprising 5.8 million men, 3.7 million women, and 1.2 million children. TB is present in all countries and age groups.

Multidrug-resistant TB remains a significant public health crisis, with only about 2 in 5 people with drug-resistant TB accessing treatment in 2024. Global efforts to combat TB have saved approximately 83 million lives since 2000. TB is preventable and curable.

Overview

Tuberculosis is an infectious disease caused by bacteria that primarily affects the lungs and spreads through the air when infected people cough, sneeze, or spit.

Approximately one-quarter of the global population is estimated to have TB bacterial infection. People with TB infection typically don't feel sick and aren't contagious. About 5-10% of infected individuals eventually develop TB disease symptoms. Babies and children face higher disease development risks if infected.

In certain countries, the Bacille Calmette-Guérin vaccine is administered to babies or young children for TB prevention. The vaccine prevents deaths and protects children from severe TB forms.

Conditions increasing TB disease risk include diabetes, weakened immune systems from HIV, undernutrition, tobacco use, and alcohol misuse.

Symptoms

TB disease occurs when bacteria multiply and affect different body organs. Symptoms may be mild for months, facilitating spread to others unknowingly.

Common TB symptoms include: prolonged cough (sometimes with blood); chest pain; weakness; fatigue; weight loss; fever; and night sweats. While TB typically affects the lungs, it can involve the kidneys, brain, spine, and skin.

Some TB patients have no symptoms but can still transmit the disease.

Prevention

Prevention measures include seeking medical attention for prolonged cough, fever, and unexplained weight loss, as early treatment for TB can help stop the spread of disease and improve your chances of recovery.

TB preventive treatment prevents infection from becoming disease. If prescribed, complete the full course. Infected individuals should practice good hygiene when coughing, avoid contact with others, wear masks, and dispose of sputum and tissues properly.

Healthcare facilities require special measures like respirators and well-ventilated spaces to reduce infection risk.

Diagnosis

WHO recommends rapid diagnostic tests as initial tests for persons with TB signs and symptoms. These include biomarker-based point-of-care tests and molecular assays, providing initial results within 48 hours of sample collection.

Tuberculin skin tests, interferon gamma release assays, and newer antigen-based skin tests identify TB infection. Results help identify individuals requiring TB preventive treatment.

Treatment

TB disease is treated with special antibiotics including rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol. Medications need to be taken daily for 4–6 months to be effective.

Stopping medications early risks TB bacteria becoming antibiotic-resistant. Drug-resistant TB requires different medicines. Multidrug-resistant TB doesn't respond to the two most effective first-line TB drugs but remains treatable with other medications, though they're more expensive with greater side effects.

In 2022, WHO guidelines prioritized a short 6-month all-oral regimen as treatment choice for eligible patients, substantially increasing treatment uptake globally.

TB and HIV

People with HIV are 12 times more likely to develop TB disease. TB is the leading cause of death among people with HIV. About 150,000 people died from HIV-associated TB in 2024.
