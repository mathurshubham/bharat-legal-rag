HYPERTENSION — WHO FACT SHEET

Key Facts

An estimated 1.4 billion adults aged 30–79 years worldwide had hypertension in 2024, representing 33% of the population in this age range. Two-thirds of those affected live in low- and middle-income countries. Approximately 600 million adults with hypertension remain unaware of their condition, while 630 million are diagnosed and treated. Only about 320 million adults have their hypertension under control. The condition represents a major cause of premature death worldwide, and WHO targets a 25% reduction in uncontrolled hypertension prevalence between 2010 and 2025.

Overview

Hypertension occurs when blood vessel pressure reaches 140/90 mmHg or higher. Most people experience no symptoms, making regular blood pressure checks essential for detection.

Risk factors include older age, family history, excess weight, sedentary lifestyle, high-salt diet, and alcohol consumption. Lifestyle modifications such as improved nutrition, tobacco cessation, and increased physical activity can lower blood pressure, though some individuals require medication.

Blood pressure consists of two measurements: systolic pressure (when the heart contracts) and diastolic pressure (when the heart rests between beats). A hypertension diagnosis requires systolic readings of 140 mmHg or higher and/or diastolic readings of 90 mmHg or higher, measured on separate occasions.

Risk Factors

Modifiable factors include unhealthy diets with excessive salt and saturated fats, insufficient fruit and vegetable intake, physical inactivity, tobacco and alcohol use, and excess weight. Air pollution represents the most significant environmental risk factor.

Non-modifiable factors include family history of hypertension, age exceeding 65 years, and conditions like diabetes or kidney disease.

Symptoms

Most hypertensive individuals feel no symptoms. Severely elevated blood pressure typically exceeding 180/120 mmHg can cause severe headaches, chest pain, dizziness, difficulty breathing, nausea, vomiting, blurred vision or other vision changes, anxiety, confusion, buzzing in the ears, nosebleeds, and abnormal heart rhythm. Seek immediate care if experiencing these symptoms alongside high blood pressure.

Health professionals must measure blood pressure to detect hypertension; home monitoring devices assist but professional evaluation remains important for comprehensive risk assessment.

Treatment

Lifestyle modifications include adopting a low-salt, healthy diet, achieving weight loss, increasing physical activity, and quitting tobacco. Doctors may prescribe medications depending on individual circumstances.

Blood pressure targets differ: less than 130/80 mmHg for those with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, or high cardiovascular risk; less than 140/90 for most others.

Common medications include ACE inhibitors, angiotensin-2 receptor blockers, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics, each functioning through different mechanisms to reduce blood pressure.

Prevention

Lifestyle changes prevent and lower hypertension. Recommended actions include consuming more vegetables and fruits, reducing sedentary time, engaging in 150 minutes weekly of moderate aerobic activity or 75 minutes of vigorous activity, performing strength exercises twice weekly, achieving healthy weight, taking prescribed medicines, and maintaining healthcare appointments.

Avoid excessive salt consumption (stay under 2 grams daily), foods high in saturated or trans fats, tobacco use, and excessive alcohol (maximum 1 drink daily for women, 2 for men).

Additional protective measures involve stress management, regular blood pressure monitoring, treating existing hypertension, managing other medical conditions, and reducing air pollution exposure.

Complications

Uncontrolled hypertension damages the heart by hardening arteries and reducing blood and oxygen flow, causing angina, heart attack, heart failure, and irregular heartbeat. The condition can also rupture or block cerebral arteries, resulting in stroke, and damage kidneys, potentially causing kidney failure.
