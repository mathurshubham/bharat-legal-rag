ANAEMIA — WHO FACT SHEET

Key Facts

Anaemia primarily impacts young children, pregnant and postpartum women, and menstruating adolescent girls and women as a major public health concern. Low- and lower-middle income countries experience the greatest burden, particularly in rural populations, poorer households, and those without formal education. Globally, approximately 40% of children aged 6–59 months, 37% of pregnant women, and 30% of women aged 15–49 years experience anaemia. In 2019, anaemia caused 50 million years of healthy life lost due to disability, with primary causes being dietary iron deficiency, thalassaemia, sickle cell trait, and malaria.

Overview

Anaemia is a condition characterized by lower-than-normal red blood cell counts or haemoglobin concentration. It occurs when insufficient haemoglobin exists to transport oxygen to organs and tissues.

Severe cases can impair cognitive and motor development in children and create complications during pregnancy and childbirth. Iron deficiency and malaria represent the most commonly recognized causes in low- and lower-middle income settings.

The condition is preventable and treatable.

Signs and Symptoms

Common symptoms include: fatigue and tiredness; dizziness or lightheadedness; cold hands and feet; headaches; shortness of breath, particularly during exertion.

Severe anaemia may cause: pale mucous membranes in the mouth and nose; pale skin and fingernails; rapid breathing and elevated heart rate; dizziness upon standing; increased bruising tendency.

Causes

Anaemia diagnosis relies on blood haemoglobin concentrations falling below established thresholds based on age, sex, and physiological status.

Nutritional Deficiencies: Iron deficiency from inadequate dietary intake represents the most common nutritional cause. Deficiencies in vitamin A, folate, vitamin B12, and riboflavin also contribute.

Blood Loss and Nutrient Losses: Sources include parasitic infections, childbirth hemorrhage, and menstrual losses.

Infections: Malaria, tuberculosis, HIV, and parasitic infections can impair nutrient absorption and metabolism or cause nutrient loss.

Chronic Diseases: Various chronic conditions trigger inflammation leading to anaemia of inflammation or anaemia of chronic disease.

Inherited Disorders: Regional prevalence of inherited red blood cell disorders includes thalassemia, sickle cell disorders, and other haemoglobinopathies.

Treatment and Prevention

Treatment and prevention strategies depend on identifying the underlying cause.

Dietary Modifications: Consume iron-rich foods including lean red meats, fish, poultry, legumes, fortified cereals, and dark leafy greens. Eat vitamin C-rich fruits and vegetables to enhance iron absorption. Avoid foods inhibiting iron absorption such as bran, tea, coffee, cocoa, and calcium when consuming iron-rich foods.

Managing Contributing Conditions: Prevent and treat malaria; address parasitic infections; maintain vaccination status; manage chronic diseases; space pregnancies at least 24 months apart and use contraception.
