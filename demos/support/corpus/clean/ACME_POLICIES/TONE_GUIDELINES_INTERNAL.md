# Support Tone & De-escalation Guide

**Doc ID:** TONE_GUIDELINES_INTERNAL | **Effective:** 2026-01-10 | **Audience:** Support agents — do not share with customers

---

## Core Principles

These four principles govern every customer interaction, regardless of channel or severity.

**1. Acknowledge before you act.**
Never jump straight to a resolution. A customer who feels unheard will escalate even when the answer is correct. Name the frustration first, then move to facts.

**2. Never argue. Ever.**
Even when the customer is factually wrong, arguing loses. Your job is to guide them to the right outcome, not to win. Redirect, don't rebut.

**3. Own the experience, not just the ticket.**
You represent Acme Tasks, not a policy document. If a rule produces a bad outcome, use your judgment (up to the $500 agent-discretion limit on refunds) and flag it for review. Don't hide behind process.

**4. Escalate early on hard stops.**
Billing disputes over $200, legal threats, P1 outages, and PII requests all require escalation. Don't attempt to resolve these solo — loop in the appropriate team immediately and tell the customer you're doing so. Silence during escalation is the fastest way to lose trust.

## Empathy Phrases

Use these as starting points — adapt to your voice. Never copy-paste robotically; customers can tell.

**Opening acknowledgment**
- "That sounds genuinely frustrating, and I'm glad you reached out."
- "I completely understand why that would catch you off guard."
- "You're right to flag this — let me look into it with you."

**When a customer is angry**
- "I hear you. Let's slow down and figure out exactly what happened."
- "I wouldn't be happy either in that situation. Here's what I can do right now."
- "Thank you for being direct with me — it helps me help you faster."

**When you need time to investigate**
- "I want to give you an accurate answer rather than a fast wrong one — can I take two minutes to check?"
- "I'm pulling up your account now. Bear with me for just a moment."

**When delivering bad news**
- "I wish I had a different answer here, and I want to be straight with you about why."
- "This isn't the outcome you were hoping for, and I get that. Here's the full picture."

**Closing**
- "Is there anything else I can clarify before we wrap up?"
- "I want to make sure you're leaving this conversation with everything you need."

## De-escalation Playbook

### Refused Refund

The current refund policy (effective 2026-01-01) covers prorated annual refunds within 30 days. Monthly plans and add-ons are non-refundable. Do **not** quote the old 14-day / full-refund policy — it was superseded and is no longer valid.

- Acknowledge the frustration before citing policy.
- Explain the *why* briefly: "Annual plans are refunded on a prorated basis because..."
- If the amount is under $500 and the situation warrants it, apply agent discretion and document your reasoning in the ticket.
- If the dispute exceeds $200 and you cannot resolve it, escalate immediately. Don't promise outcomes you can't guarantee.

### Outage or Degraded Service

- Acknowledge impact first: validate that their work was disrupted.
- Point them to the status page for live updates; don't speculate on cause or timeline.
- For Business/Enterprise customers, confirm their SLA tier (99.9% or 99.95%) and note that credit eligibility will be assessed automatically post-incident.
- P1 outages are an immediate escalation trigger — loop in the on-call team before responding further.
- Never say "everything looks fine on our end" without verifying.

### Feature Missing or Not Working as Expected

- Confirm whether it's a bug, a tier limitation, or a misunderstanding before responding.
- If it's a tier limitation (e.g., SSO is Business+, audit log export is Enterprise-only), be honest and direct — don't make the customer feel foolish for not knowing.
- Offer a concrete next step: upgrade path, workaround, or a feature request submission.
- Never promise a feature will ship by a specific date. Roadmap items are confidential.

### Billing Surprise

- Assume good faith. Most billing surprises are seat overages, mid-cycle upgrades, or annual renewal timing.
- Walk through the charge line by line. Transparency dissolves most of these quickly.
- If the customer disputes a charge over $200, escalate per policy — do not attempt to negotiate unilaterally.

## Sample Scripts

### Refused Refund (Monthly Plan)

> "I completely understand the frustration here — unexpected charges are stressful. I've reviewed your account, and your subscription is on a monthly plan. Under our current billing policy, monthly subscriptions aren't eligible for refunds after the billing cycle processes. I know that's not what you were hoping to hear. What I *can* do is make sure your account is set to cancel at the end of this cycle so there are no further charges, and I'm happy to walk you through any plan changes that might work better going forward. Does that help?"

### Outage Acknowledgment

> "I'm really sorry your team hit this during [time period] — I can see from our records that there was a service disruption, and I understand how much that impacts your work. Our engineering team is actively on it, and you can track live updates at our status page. Once the incident is resolved, our team will assess SLA credit eligibility automatically for affected Business and Enterprise accounts. I'll make a note on your account so nothing falls through the cracks. Is there anything else I can do for you right now?"

### Feature Not on Their Tier

> "Great question — I want to be upfront with you. SSO is included on our Business plan and above, so it isn't available on your current Pro subscription. That's not a bug, just a tier difference, and I realize it can be frustrating to hit that wall mid-workflow. If it's something your team needs, I'm happy to walk you through what the Business plan includes and what an upgrade would look like. No pressure — just want to make sure you have the full picture."

### Billing Surprise (Seat Overage)

> "Thanks for flagging this — let me pull up the details so we can look at it together. It looks like your workspace added two seats on [date], which triggered a prorated charge for the remainder of the billing period. That's the most common reason for an unexpected line item. Does that match what you're seeing on your end? If something still doesn't add up, I want to make sure we get to the bottom of it."
